Body of nano-plier


Thomas Gerling (2016) produced a structure of pliers in nano-scale with one rotation degree of freedom, by using DNA origami[1]. We applied his structure, and combined six bundles of alpha helix to create the nano-plier. The Nano-plier's total length is 300 nm. At 200 nm from the end of “handle”, the photoresponsive switch is incorporated, and the aptamer is attached at 75 nm from end of “blade.” At 25nm is a joint part. The angle between the blades is assumed to be 90° under initial state, and when the nano-plier is closed, the angle is assumed to be 60°.

Force of more than 1 nN is required to destroy the membrane of influenza viruses. However, the existing force scale of the hybridization is on the pN scale. Therefore, we propose complementary strand pairs to generate force of more than 1 nN with the nano-plier. There were two conditions to achieve it: force of 50 pN was required to pull apart the 10.2 nm complementary sequences[2](Kufer,2008), and the structure is a rigid body such that the leverage principle works. The validity of being rigid is discussed in the result section. A pair of complementary sequences appear at every 10 nm on the stranded on the “handle” part (the 200 nm part). The length of each sequence should correspond to the distances between the blade when the plier is closed. The force on the virus end of the plier can be calculated as an equation (1) and is approximately 14nN. (87.5nm comes from the “blade” part plus half of the joint part)

Aptamer

There were two options to bind influenza viruses: antibodies and nucleic acid molecules. Antibodies are complicated and costly to prepare. Moreover, they bind to other antigens as well as the target, so the improvement of detection accuracy was a problem to overcome. Owing to these problems, nucleic acid molecules which specifically bind to an antigen have recently been gathering attention. Such nucleic acid molecules are called aptamers and applied it in our nano-plier. The aptamer is capable of binding to hemagglutinin (HA) that came from influenza virus[3]. Specifically, it binds to HA and HA subunits such as HA1 and HA1/H2, which are complexed with each other. It can bind harmful types of influenza viruses. 　　　

The aptamer is a polynucleotide consisting of the following sequences;

GTGTTGTTGATGGTTTGGTCTGGTTGGCTTAACAC

The estimated secondary structure of polynucleotides is shown in Fig.Aptamers were anchored to the nano-plier by using a type of vitamin called biotin and a protein called streptavidin that binds to biotin. The binding of streptavidin to the end of the plier and biotin to the 3' end of the aptamer led to the binding of the aptamer to the plier.

Photoresponsive switch

Azobenzene generates force while changing from cis to trans when exposed to visible spectrum.To use this, we introduced an azobenzene into the DNA single-strand and binded it to the complementary sequence.About 70% of the complementary single strand of DNA at a distance of about 70 nm was bound when exposed to the visible spectrum for 5 minutes (Takenaka,2014)[4]. This study suggests that exposing the plier to the visible spectrum for a long time may result in a high probability of plier closing.
