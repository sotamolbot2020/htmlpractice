Simulation Result


In the method section, based on the assumption that the nano-plier is rigid and not deformed by the application of force, the force generated by hybridization was amplified and acted on the virus according to the leverage principle. However, nano-plier, which is soft and made up of DNA, is deformed by the force. Therefore, it is thought that the force amplification using the leverage principle does not work much as a rigid body. In this simulation, we used structural analysis to evaluate the effect of nano-pliers' deformation on the forces acting on the virus.

Simulation Consideration

The modeling and static simulation of the nano-plier was done using Fusion 360. The model was designed on a millimeter-scale (Fig). Finite element analysis used in the static simulations is a multiscale method, and therefore the results obtained by the milli-scale model can be used for a nanoscale structure, except whether the nanoscale structure can be considered as a continuum is not obvious. The physical properties of the DNA origami and the model of the six bundles of helix are based on the work of Dominik J. Kauert (2011)[1]. The Young's modulus for bending is 261 MPa, the Poisson's ratio is 0.25 and the DNA origami is isotropic, and the model of the six bundles of α helix consists of a cylinder and connectors (Fig). The plane at the end of the virus catcher side was constrained vertically, and the area where the two pliers crossed was pin-constrained (Fig. 6). Two types of loading were tested; a total of 2 N was applied to the upper cylinder on the strand side of the DNA plier, and 2 nN was applied near the tip.
